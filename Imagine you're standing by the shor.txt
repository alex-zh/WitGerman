"Why do people fear death?"

Is it the fear of the unknown, the loss of loved ones, or something else? And can this fear be overcome?



Imagine you're standing by the shore of a lake, and you see a boy drowning. He's calling for help, and you have the chance to save him. The boy's name is Adolf, and he will grow up to become Adolf Hitler. Would you save him?


1. Conflict Between Universal Morality and the Consequences of Actions
Universal morality: Saving a life is considered the morally right thing to do. Refusing to help the boy in this situation could be seen as immoral.
Consequences: However, saving the boy means he might grow up to become Hitler, responsible for horrifying crimes. This raises the question: is it more important to act on the principle of "saving everyone" or to evaluate long-term consequences?

2. Predetermination and Free Will
If you know the boy will become a dictator, does that mean his future is already predetermined?
Could the boy grow up to be a different person if his life took another path? In that case, letting him die (or refusing to save him) would not be justified but cruel.

3. Personal Involvement and Responsibility
If you decide not to save the boy, does that mean you are guilty of his death?
On the other hand, if you save him, does that make you partially responsible for his future crimes?

4. Emotional Dilemma
Even knowing the future, refusing to help a drowning child could feel morally and emotionally unbearable. This creates an inner conflict between humanity and rational calculation.

5. Historical Dilemma
If you don’t save the boy, does that mean you are changing the course of history? Could this lead to even worse events, where another, even more ruthless leader takes his place?
Or conversely, would refusing to save him prevent millions of deaths?

6. Complexity in the Context of Punishment and Justice
Is it fair to "punish" a child for actions he has not yet committed? After all, at this moment, he is innocent.
